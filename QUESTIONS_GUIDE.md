# MindFlow Question Authoring Guide

## Core Philosophy

MindFlow measures fundamental psychological preferences, not industry exposure or corporate vocabulary. Questions should reveal a user's natural workstyle, attention patterns, decision preferences, and collaboration habits without requiring executive, startup, or management experience.

## Universal Operations Principle

A question must make immediate sense to a retail supervisor, a university student, a salesperson, an office worker, a software engineer, a part-time worker, a volunteer team member, and a founder or manager.

Write for universal operations: the recurring human situations that appear in many settings, such as unclear instructions, competing priorities, collaboration, feedback, deadlines, problem solving, planning, and adapting when conditions change.

## Writing Rules

- Isolate the cognitive mechanism, not the setting.
- Maintain professional dignity and an adult reflective tone.
- Keep both answer options equally respectable.
- Use grammatical parallelism so one option does not sound more responsible or mature than the other.
- Preserve the underlying psychometric contrast:
  - Sensing vs. Intuition: concrete details and direct evidence vs. broader meaning and patterns over time.
  - Thinking vs. Feeling: clear logic and objective checkpoints vs. relational safety and shared understanding.
  - Judging vs. Perceiving: closing loops and setting standards vs. leaving room to adapt as things change.
  - Extraversion vs. Introversion: engaging broadly first vs. thinking quietly first before engaging.
- Avoid slang, emojis, childish phrasing, and overly casual wording.
- Avoid clinical, diagnostic, IQ, disorder, or pathology wording.
- Avoid corporate jargon unless the whole app is explicitly targeting executives.

## Banned vs. Approved

| Banned or Narrow Wording | Approved Universal Alternative |
| --- | --- |
| stakeholder | different groups of people; people involved; people affected |
| deliverable | important task; project; outcome |
| briefing | instructions; context; update |
| proposal | new idea; planned approach; option |
| launching | starting something important |
| workflow optimization | finding a better way to complete a recurring task |
| escalation | sudden problem that forces a change of plans |
| bandwidth | time and focus |
| alignment | shared understanding; agreement; getting everyone on the same page |
| synergy | working well together; combined effort |
| deploy | put into use; roll out; start using |
| mapping | understanding; outlining; summarizing |
| board room | meeting; group discussion |
| Q3 | a future period; the next phase; later in the year |
| metrics review | progress review; reviewing results |

## Examples

### Ambiguity

Avoid: “In ambiguous briefings, your first question is usually...”

Use: “When instructions are vague or open-ended, your first question is usually...”

Balanced options:

- “What do we know from direct evidence?”
- “What meaning might this suggest over time?”

### Collaboration

Avoid: “In stakeholder mapping, you are more likely to...”

Use: “When working with different people, you are more likely to...”

Balanced options:

- “Speak with a wider range of people first, then summarize what you learned.”
- “Build your own understanding first, then speak with people selectively.”

### Execution

Avoid: “For major deliverables, you prefer to...”

Use: “For important tasks, you prefer to...”

Balanced options:

- “Set milestones early and track against them.”
- “Preserve room for new insight as the situation evolves.”

## Cognitive and Working-Memory Items

Cognitive and working-memory prompts should spend the user's effort on solving the task, not decoding unclear wording. These items are still non-diagnostic reflection samples, so clarity and reassurance matter as much as brevity.

- Use complete, active sentences instead of shorthand or broken phrasing.
- Avoid abbreviations that make the user reconstruct the task before solving it.
- Formula questions may include a high-level method hint when appropriate, such as naming the relationship before asking the user to compare answer choices.
- Spatial or reversal questions should ask for one clear target and keep answer options consistent with that target.
- Timed memory screens must visually and semantically separate the content to study from the countdown timer.
- “I don’t know” is acceptable and should not be framed as failure; it is a safe option when a reasoning or recall item feels unclear or out of reach.
- Preserve cognitive correctness logic, delayed recall architecture, IDs, scoring weights, and scoring metadata unless a dedicated scoring change is planned and reviewed.

## PR Checklist for Question Changes

- Active English question strings contain no unnecessary corporate jargon.
- No question IDs changed.
- No answer option values changed.
- No scoring weights changed.
- No axes, dimensions, group labels, scoring domains, or scoring directions changed.
- No scoring formulas changed.
- Both answer options are balanced and equally respectable.
- The count display is dynamic and matches the active session length.
- Cognitive timing, memory behavior, session randomization, report layout, and PDF export are unchanged unless the PR explicitly targets those areas.

## Metadata Safety Warning

Question copy may be edited, but IDs, weights, axes, option values, scoring domains, scoring directions, timing metadata, and all other scoring metadata must remain stable unless a dedicated scoring migration is planned and reviewed.
